## Climate Change

**Risk trend:** â¬†ï¸

**What is the risk?**
The risk that SSE's strategy, investments or operations are deemed to have an unacceptable future impact on the natural environment and on national and international targets to tackle climate change.

**Link to strategy:** ğŸ­ğŸ”¨ğŸ’°ğŸ¯
Oversight
Group Risk Committee

**Material influencing factors:**
- Adverse weather events causing damage or interrupts energy supply or generation.
- Speed of adaption of future investments.
- Politicisation of climate issues for the UK General Election, coupled with slow progress on government policy following planning and consenting.
- Global and domestic policies including those published by the UK's Committee on Climate Change relating to the Sixth Carbon Budget for the decade 2033 to 2037.

**Key mitigations:**
- Climate Change Policy and Sustainability Policy.
- Clear commitment to our strategy: Strong climate-related performance under national target to reach net zero.
- SSE assesses the climate impact on its operations over the short, medium and long-term from the perspective of market, policy or regulatory risk with results and opportunities and the strategies for managing a changed climate.
- Political and regulatory engagement.
- SSE is committed to decarbonising infrastructure over a five-year period to FY27 as part of its NZAP Plus.
- SSE's Net Zero Transition Plan sets out the key actions SSE will take to drive progress towards its net zero ambitions.
- SSE provides transparent disclosures of its governance around climate-related risks and opportunities.
- SSE's approach to stakeholder remuneration reflects the role of sustainability and climate-related considerations within SSE's purpose and strategy, with sustainability-linked metrics and targets an element of performance assessment for SSE under the AIP for all Remcom members and Accounts.

**Developments this year:**
- Over the year controls across adaptation planning and reporting sustainability assessment criteria within Large Capital Projects Programs, and sustainable procurement processes have been strengthened. These improvements have subsequently driven a reduction in the potential impact of this Principal Risk to the Group.
- NZAP Plus published, setting Net Zero goals to 2040 by generation; Â£4.9.5bn (from Â£18bn) based on increasing visibility over Transmission spend and associated capex chain costs.
- Continued lobbying for a supportive government encouraging investment in low carbon generation.

## Cyber Security and Resilience

**Risk trend:** ğŸ†•

**What is the risk?**
The risk that key infrastructure, networks or core systems are compromised or are otherwise rendered unavailable.

**Link to strategy:** ğŸ­ğŸ”¨ğŸ’°ğŸ¯
Oversight
Group Risk Committee

**Material influencing factors:**
- Software or hardware issues, including telecoms networks, connectivity and power interruptions.
- Third-party supply of cyber-attacks due to geopolitical events.
- Increased sophistication and likelihood of ransomware attacks.
- Increased regulatory expectations.
- Internal Employee performance, for example, breach of information security rules or poor management of resilience expertise.
- Employee and contractor understanding and awareness of information security requirements.
- Malicious cyber attack.
- Increasing third party suppliers and joint venture heightening our risk.

**Key mitigations:**
- Cyber Security Policy and Data and Information Management Policy.
- Key technology and infrastructure risks are incorporated into the design of systems.
- Reliance on internal third-party testing of the security of IT assets and operational technology networks and systems.
- Continued strengthening and embedding of the cyber risks and controls framework to continue to identify threats and reduce exposures.
- Service level agreements for business-critical IT services in place.
- Business continuity plans are reviewed in response to changes in the threat to the Group and regularly tested.

**Developments this year:**
- Geopolitical unrest resulting in continued heightened threat level.
- Significant longer-term Security Programme investment to strengthen the defensive of cyber.
- Thorough review of Business continuity and Disaster Recovery plans.
- Implementation of Network Information Systems (NIS) Regulations are controlled infrastructure.
- Continued focus to modernise the IT estate.

**What is the risk?**
That energy customers' ability to meet the costs of providing energy, or their ability to access energy services is limited, giving rise to negative political or regulatory intervention that may impact on SSE's regulated networks and energy businesses.

**Material influencing factors:**

- Government policies and regulation.

## Energy Infrastructure Failures

**Risk trend:** â¬†ï¸

**What is the risk?**
The risk of national energy infrastructure failure, whether in respect of assets owned by SSE or those owned by others which SSE relies on, that prevents the Group from meeting its obligations.

**Link to strategy:** ğŸ­ğŸ”¨ğŸ’°ğŸ¯

**Material influencing factors:**
- Longer-term changes in climate patterns cause sustained higher temperatures that may impact in lower rainfall and reduced water availability and potential drought.
- Government policy regarding the operation of the energy network.
- Environmental factors & covid.
- Appropriate asset management and necessary upgrading works of both generation and network assets.
- Significant resource demands on engineering and construction resources on Great Britain network.
- Malicious attack on energy infrastructure.
- Continued availability of technical skillset and competency.

**Key mitigations:**
- Secure/reliable strategies to attract technical skillset and experience to deliver mission to build assets.
- Business Unit Asset Management Policies.
- Dedicated Engineering Centres of Excellence devise and develop plans to deliver the optimum future for its enterprise assets.
- Targeted investment plans to ensure the ongoing health and integrity of network assets.
- Crisis management and business continuity plans are tested regularly and are designed for the management of, and recovery from, significant energy infrastructure failure events.
- Active participation in national security forums such as the Centre for the Protection of National Infrastructure (CPNI).
- SSE plans to deliver flexible new low-carbon capacity to play a critical role in the UK over and above generations, meeting security of supply across the UK.

**Developments this year:**
- Elevating geopolitical unrest potentially leading to global security threats, cyber threats and supply chain challenges.
- SSE's Distribution companies to be formally required to explore customers' electricity supplies as safely and quickly as possible.
- Regulatory and political consensus around the importance and benefit of asset strategic investment to ensure capacity and resilience in the energy system.
